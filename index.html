Introduction

CAPA is not paperwork. It's a regulatory commitment.

Every Malaysian GMP and pharmaceutical framework treats CAPA as a non-negotiable element of the quality management system — not a box to tick after a finding.

NPRA — CAPA System Guidance, 2016

The CAPA system is one of the most important elements of a quality management system, applied to determine root cause across complaints, rejections, non-conformances, recalls, deviations, audits and process trends.

NPRA-DRGD Appendix 34 — Product Quality Reporting

NPRA oversees investigations into product quality defects in the Malaysian market and expects companies to monitor CAPA effectiveness through regular trend analysis.

TMHS GMP Guidelines — Self-Inspection 9.1

Self-inspections must monitor GMP compliance, identify areas for improvement, and propose corrective measures as a continuous discipline.

Without effective CAPA
  • Repeat failures and the same complaints recur
  • Persistent audit findings and non-conformances
  • Risk of product recall or market withdrawal
  • Loss of customer and regulatory trust
  • Resources wasted resolving the same issue twice
With effective CAPA
  • Fewer repeat problems — quality improves over time
  • Confident, evidence-backed audit responses
  • Safer, more consistent products for consumers
  • A stronger quality culture across the organisation
  • Measurable improvement in operational productivity

The cascade when a CAPA fails

A weak CAPA rarely stays contained to one finding — it tends to compound.

1

Escalated regulatory action

A rejected CAPA response can trigger an NPRA Warning Letter, a downgraded GMP certificate, or a costly follow-up inspection.

2

Findings become "systemic"

A recurrence at the next audit gets reclassified as a quality-system failure — a far more serious category than the original finding.

3

Recall or market action

If the root cause wasn't genuinely eliminated, recurrence on released product can trigger a recall or field alert.

4

Lost client relationships

For OEM contract manufacturers, a weak CAPA response to a client audit can mean losing the contract outright — clients audit suppliers specifically to test CAPA maturity.

5

Certificate suspension

In severe or repeated cases, NPRA can suspend or revoke a manufacturing licence, halting production until remediation is verified.

6

Erosion of quality culture

When CAPAs are written to "close the file," staff learn documentation is a formality — and real risks go unaddressed.

🎓 Auditor's note — beyond the source

Correction vs. Corrective Action vs. Preventive Action — the three terms get blurred constantly, and auditors notice. A correction fixes the immediate symptom (re-sign the missing BMR field). A corrective action fixes the root cause so the same gap can't recur (revise SOP-QA-012, add an escalation path). A preventive action looks sideways and forward — does this same gap exist in other products, lines, or sites before it's ever found there? A CAPA that only contains a correction is not a CAPA; it's an excuse with a reference number.

Module 1 · What Starts the Cycle

What triggers a CAPA?

Any quality event that reveals an existing or potential failure in a product, process or system can open a CAPA.

▸ Audit Finding

NPRA regulatory inspection, internal GMP audit, or client/third-party audit — any classified finding (Critical, Major, Minor).

▸ Deviation

Machine breakdown, temperature excursion, missed in-process step, or any unplanned departure from an approved procedure.

▸ Customer Complaint

Leaking container, foreign material, substandard performance — any complaint indicating a quality or safety risk.

▸ OOS / OOT Result

pH, assay, microbial count, or any test result falling outside specification or alert/action limits.

▸ Supplier Issue

Raw material or packaging failing specification, including CoA discrepancies or non-conforming deliveries.

▸ Trend Analysis

A pattern of repeated minor deviations or complaints that individually wouldn't trigger a CAPA but collectively signal systemic risk.

🎓 Auditor's note — beyond the source

With limited QA headcount, not every trigger deserves the same depth of CAPA. Score incoming findings on a simple Risk Priority Number — severity × likelihood × detectability — and let that score set the investigation depth and RCA tool, not your gut feel or how busy the week is. A backlog of open CAPAs sitting past their target date is one of the first things an NPRA or client auditor will pull up; treat CAPA aging as a KPI your QA team reviews monthly, not just the closure count.

Module 1 · CAPA Workflow

The seven steps, in practice

Each step below follows the worked example used throughout this manual: CAPA-2026-017 — a Major NPRA finding for missing dual sign-off on three batches of ABC Capsules.

01

Identification

What & why

Formal recording of an audit finding — from a regulator, internal audit, or client — as a CAPA trigger. Every finding carrying risk to quality, compliance, or patient safety must be logged with a clear, factual description before any other step begins.

Key activities
  • Log the finding in the CAPA and non-conformance register
  • Record the auditor's wording verbatim — never paraphrase
  • Assign a unique CAPA reference linked to the audit report
  • Classify as Critical / Major / Minor / Observation
  • Assign an owner with real authority over the affected process
  • Confirm and calendar the response deadline
Worked example

Finding: "Second reviewer sign-off absent for Batches X, Y, Z. BMR 4.3 requires dual sign-off. Classification: MAJOR."
CAPA-2026-017 raised. Owner: QA Manager. Deadline: 30 days from close-out.

Common mistakes
  • Paraphrasing the finding instead of recording exact wording
  • Raising the CAPA days late — delay signals poor quality culture
  • Merging multiple findings into one CAPA reference
02

Evaluation

Scope the real risk

A structured risk assessment of the finding's true scope, its impact on product quality and patient safety, and any regulatory reporting obligation. Confirms whether immediate containment is needed.

Key questions
  • Isolated incident, or a systemic quality gap?
  • How many products, batches or sites are affected?
  • Does it require regulatory notification or a recall assessment?
  • Has any affected product already reached the market?
Worked example

BMR review of all Q3 batches found 3 of 30 affected. All 3 already distributed; in-process data showed no OOS results. Patient safety risk: LOW. Recall not warranted — rationale documented. Batch release suspended pending SOP review.

03

Investigation

Gather the facts

Objective fact-finding before any conclusion is drawn — BMR review, SOP review, personnel interviews, and training records, not assumptions.

Worked example

BMR: Section 4.3 blank in 3 batches only. SOP-QA-012 confirmed two signatures required. Interview: second reviewer was absent with no cover arranged. Training: supervisor's last refresher was 2021; new analyst joined 2023 with no handover.

Common mistakes
  • Limiting review to the finding itself — not checking if it occurred elsewhere or earlier
  • Accepting the first explanation without verifying against records
  • Not reviewing previous audit reports for repeat findings
04

Root Cause Analysis

Why did the system allow this?

RCA asks why the quality system failed to prevent or detect the problem — process gaps, training deficiencies, oversight failures, never personal blame. See the full RCA Toolkit further down this manual.

Worked example — 5-Why chain

Missing sign-off → reviewer absent, no cover → no deputy-reviewer procedure → SOP silent on escalation → SOP not reviewed after 2023 restructure → root cause: no systematic SOP-impact review triggered by organisational change.

05

Action Plan

What will be done, by whom, by when

A formal, time-bound plan tracing every action directly back to the confirmed root cause — corrective actions fix the cause; preventive actions check for the same gap elsewhere; horizontal-spread checks confirm nothing else is hiding the same failure.

Common mistakes
  • Actions that target the symptom — e.g. "remind staff to sign"
  • All actions given the same unrealistic deadline
  • Training scheduled before the revised SOP is even approved
06

Implementation

Execute, and prove it

Every committed action is carried out and documented with objective evidence. Implementation is not complete until that evidence is filed and retrievable — the auditor will ask for it at the next inspection.

Evidence expected
  • Revised, approved SOP with version number and effective date
  • Signed training attendance register with SOP version trained
  • Change control closure certificate referencing all linked actions
Common mistakes
  • Marking an action complete with no objective evidence attached
  • Training staff on a draft SOP instead of the approved version
  • Evidence left in personal inboxes instead of the QMS
07

Verification & Closure

Prove it stayed fixed

An objective, evidence-based check — conducted after full implementation, typically 60–90 days minimum — to confirm the root cause stayed eliminated. Only after a positive check can the CAPA be formally closed and the auditor notified.

Worked example — verdict

Criteria: zero missing sign-offs across the next 10 batches; ≥95% monthly spot-check pass rate at 60 and 90 days. Result: all 10 BMRs compliant, both spot-checks passed. VERDICT: EFFECTIVE — CAPA closed, evidence filed in QMS, NPRA notified.

Common mistakes
  • Verifying too soon — two weeks is not enough for a systemic finding
  • Closure criteria defined as "no repeat finding" with no measurable threshold
  • Forgetting to notify the regulatory authority of closure when required
Module 2 · Root Cause Analysis

Choosing the right RCA tool

All three tools chase the same target — your choice depends on how complex and how cross-functional the finding is.

Tool 1

5-Why

Ask "why?" repeatedly — usually five times — until you reach a process or system layer the organisation can actually fix.

Best for: simple, linear findings · Routine/Minor classifications. Fast to complete and document.

Tool 2

Fishbone (Ishikawa)

A diagram shaped like a fish skeleton — the problem sits at the head, six cause categories branch as bones.

Best for: multi-factor findings, Major classifications, cross-functional RCA workshops.

Tool 3

6M Analysis

A fixed checklist of six categories — Man, Machine, Method, Material, Measurement, Milieu — that drives the brainstorm itself.

Best for: ensuring no cause category gets skipped. Used inside Fishbone to populate the six bones.

5-Why — the worked chain

PROBLEM
Missing dual sign-off in BMR Section 4.3 — Batches X, Y, Z
WHY 1
Second reviewer was absent — no cover arranged
WHY 2
No formal procedure for arranging a deputy reviewer during absences
WHY 3
SOP-QA-012 defines the sign-off requirement but is silent on escalation
WHY 4
SOP-QA-012 was never reviewed after the 2023 team restructure
ROOT
No systematic SOP review is triggered by organisational change — a change-management control gap

6M — the same finding, six lenses

6M provides the categories; Fishbone provides the visual structure. Even ruling a category out is a required, documented step.

Man — Contributing

No deputy arranged; supervisor misread sign-off responsibility; no refresher since 2021.

Machine — Ruled out

No equipment involved in the manual sign-off step.

Method — Root Cause

SOP defines dual sign-off but has no escalation path; never updated after restructure.

Material — Ruled out

Finding relates to process control, not material inputs.

Measurement — Ruled out

Not related to the sign-off issue.

Milieu — Root Cause

2023 restructure changed reviewer roles with no SOP-review trigger — change-control gap.

🎓 Auditor's note — beyond the source

Internationally, this is the same expectation written into ICH Q10 (Pharmaceutical Quality System) and 21 CFR 820.100 / ISO 13485 for device and combination products — root cause must address the system, and effectiveness must be objectively verified before closure. If Weryx ever supplies a brand owner who reports into FDA or EU frameworks, your CAPA file is the document that gets requested first during their supplier audit — write it to that standard even when the immediate trigger is only an internal NPRA-scope finding.

Inspection Readiness

What auditors actually check

Before writing a CAPA, picture the auditor opening this file twelve months from now with no other context. What do they check first?

They askWhy it matters
Does the problem statement match the original finding, word for word?Auditors compare your record against their original inspection notes — any drift raises questions.
Is the root cause systemic, or does it blame an individual?"Operator error" as a stated root cause is an immediate red flag signalling a shallow investigation.
Do the actions logically follow from the root cause?Auditors trace backwards from each action to the root cause — if the link isn't obvious, they will ask you to explain it.
Is there objective evidence for every completed action?A tick in a tracker is not evidence. They expect the actual SOP, training record, or audit report.
Were target dates met — and if not, was it explained?Missed deadlines without documented justification suggest the CAPA was not actively managed.
Was effectiveness verified, or just assumed?"No further complaints" is not verification — they look for a defined check, method, and result.

Module 3 · Writing & Closing a CAPA

The anatomy of a well-written CAPA

A complete CAPA record answers six questions. If any one is missing or weak, it will not withstand regulatory scrutiny.

#QuestionWhat "complete" looks like
1WHAT happened?A precise, factual problem statement — no assumptions, no blame
2WHY did it happen?Documented, evidence-based RCA using a named methodology
3WHAT will be done?Specific corrective and preventive actions with named owners
4WHO is responsible?Named individuals — not departments or generic roles
5BY WHEN?Realistic, phased target dates aligned to the response deadline
6HOW do we know it worked?Pre-defined, measurable effectiveness criteria — not "no further complaints"

Writing the problem statement — the golden rule

Describe what happened — never why, and never what will be done about it. Record the finding verbatim, then add factual context: what, where, when, how many, how bad.

✗
"Product failed GMP requirement"Too vague — no what, where, when, or extent
✓
"Dual sign-off absent in BMR Section 4.3 for Batches X, Y, Z — Q3 2025, NPRA Major Finding"Specific, factual, taken directly from the finding
✗
"Staff did not follow the SOP correctly"Already includes an assumption about cause
✓
"Section 4.3 sign-off field was blank in 3 of 30 Q3 2025 BMRs — confirmed by QA record review"Factual, scoped, evidence-based

Module 3

CAPA closure checklist

Before signing off closure, every item below must be satisfied. If any one is incomplete, the CAPA stays open.

Problem

Statement is factual, specific, and matches the audit finding verbatim

RCA

Root cause confirmed, documented and QA-approved — never "human error"

Actions

All planned actions completed with objective evidence attached

Traceability

Every action traces to the confirmed root cause — no orphan actions

Documents

All revised SOPs approved, version-controlled and formally distributed

Training

All affected staff trained on revised SOPs; records on file

Change control

Raised, actioned and formally closed

Evidence

Evidence package indexed and filed in the QMS — not personal drives or email

Effectiveness

Criteria met, evidenced, and signed off by QA

Notification

Regulatory authority or client notified of closure where required

Conclusion

Key takeaways

01

CAPA is a commitment, not a form

Every response is a written promise to the auditor. What you write will be verified — make it accurate and evidence-based.

02

Root cause is systemic, not personal

Regulators expect system failures to be named and fixed. "Human error" alone is never an acceptable root cause.

03

Evidence is everything

If it isn't documented, it didn't happen. Every completed action needs objective, retrievable evidence in the QMS.

04

Effectiveness must be measured

Define criteria before implementation, not after. A CAPA isn't closed until the root cause is demonstrably gone.

05

Timeliness signals quality culture

How quickly and thoroughly you respond tells the auditor as much as the content of the response itself.

Before you go: one commitment

Next time I receive an audit finding, before writing the problem statement I will…
  • Copy the auditor's exact wording first, before adding any interpretation
  • Confirm the scope before describing the problem
  • Check the severity classification and not downgrade it without QA approval
Next time I run an RCA, I will make sure to…
  • Keep asking "why" until I reach a system or process gap — not stop at "human error"
  • Involve someone from another department
  • Base every step on investigation evidence, not assumptions
One thing I will stop doing in a CAPA response is…
  • Writing "staff have been reminded/retrained" as the only corrective action
  • Defining effectiveness as "no further complaints" with no measurable criteria
